The Lindy Effect

By Nassim Nicholas Taleb

Definition

The Lindy Effect is a theorized phenomenon by which the future life expectancy of some non-perishable things, like a technology or an idea, is proportional to their current age. Every additional period of survival implies a longer remaining life expectancy.

Key Principles

1. Time as a Filter
   - What has survived long has proven its robustness against randomness and change.
   - The longer a book has been in print, the longer it is likely to remain in print.

2. Perishable vs. Non-Perishable
   - Perishable things (humans, animals) have an expected lifespan that decreases with age.
   - Non-perishable things (technologies, ideas, books) have an expected lifespan that increases with age.

3. Antifragility Connection
   - Things that have survived shocks and stresses become more robust over time.
   - The Lindy Effect is a manifestation of antifragility in the information domain.

Examples

- Books: Works by Seneca, Marcus Aurelius, and Shakespeare continue to be read centuries after publication.
- Technologies: The wheel, the lever, and written language have persisted for millennia.
- Cuisine: Traditional recipes that have survived generations are likely to continue.

Counter-Examples

- Fads and trends that emerge quickly often disappear just as fast.
- Technologies that solve temporary problems may not persist.

Conclusion

When evaluating the longevity of ideas, technologies, or cultural artifacts, look to their age as a predictor of their future survival. Time is the ultimate stressor, and only the robust and antifragile survive its test.
